**Abstract**: The theoretical foundations of the interactions across the space, asset, and capital markets in real estate are well established. The empirical evidence of these interactions across those three markets remains more limited. This study empirically investigates the dynamic interactions across the three markets, taking China's urban housing markets as an example. Using a panel dataset of 132 cities from 2014 to 2024, we systematically identify the asymmetric and directional interactions through panel vector autoregressions, Granger causality test, impulse response functions, and forecast error variance decompositions. The results show that the capital market functions as the vital regulatory pivot where price shocks drive a price-led yield compression mechanism, which subsequently governs corrective asset devaluation in the asset market and staggered rental escalation in the space market. Further, the growth dynamics of the space and asset markets are mutually insulated within a 10-year forecast horizon. In addition, heterogeneity analysis reveals a structural divergence that high-tier, highland supply , and highland price cities are predominantly governed by this asset-driven transmission framework, whereas other cities are dictated by a space-market channel where rent operates as the leading valuation anchor.
