**Abstract**: This study examines how regional income inequality affects individual household leverage and propagates intertemporal housing polarization through the housing market. Using American Housing Survey (AHS) micro-level panels across fifteen major U.S. metropolitan areas from 2015 to 2023, we deploy a structural two-stage least squares framework using a Bartik-type instrument to address household sorting. We document a striking divergence that rising regional Gini coefficient, rather than the share of top households, significantly reduces individual loan-to-value (LTV) ratios but expands mortgage-to-income (MTI) ratios. Mechanism analysis reveals that the LTV reduction is not driven by lenders’ risk-screening, but by a structural compositional shift where middle- and low-income households are priced out of homeownership. Concurrently, high-income households bidding up high-end housing assets fuels a mortgage expansion that drives up MTI. Furthermore, analysis based on the life cycle hypothesis reveals that initial leverage, given the housing market conditions shaped by regional income inequality, produces a gradual but substantial drag on future housing value over an eight-year horizon. By establishing a post-Great-Recession empirical baseline, these findings demonstrate that, through protecting the housing consumption of homeowner households while blocking poorer households from even entering the homeownership market, regional income inequality polarizes housing over time.
